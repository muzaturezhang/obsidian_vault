What your classmate described is actually a **very common (and very strategic) research practice**. Good researchers almost never wait until the “final big result” — they **slice their research into publishable units**.

The key idea is this:

> A paper does NOT need to be “the whole project.”  
> It only needs to be a **complete, self-contained contribution**.

---

# 🧠 First: Why this works (important mindset shift)

Think of research like a big system:

```
Big Project
├── Problem framing
├── Method A
├── Method B
├── Experiment system
├── Analysis
└── Applications
```

Each of these can become a paper if it satisfies:

✅ Has a **clear question**  
✅ Has a **non-trivial answer**  
✅ Has **evidence (experiment / proof / reasoning)**

---

# 🔍 Three common ways to “cut” a small paper

This connects directly to the 3 types you mentioned before:

---

## ✅ Type 1: “New perspective / framework” → conceptual slice

You don’t even need full results yet.

### How your 学长 might do it:

- While doing research, they realize:
    
    > “People are looking at this problem in the wrong way.”
    

So they write a paper like:

- redefine the problem
    
- propose a new framework
    
- maybe give small examples
    

### Example (ML):

- “Instead of treating this as classification, treat it as ranking”
    

💡 This is often:

- light experiments
    
- heavy thinking
    

---

## ✅ Type 2: “New comparison / synthesis” → experimental slice

This is the **easiest entry point for beginners**.

### How it works:

Take a sub-question like:

> “Which method actually works better under condition X?”

Then:

- implement several methods
    
- run controlled experiments
    
- analyze results deeply
    

### This becomes a paper:

- NOT new algorithm
    
- BUT new **insight**
    

💡 Example:

- Compare loss functions on small datasets
    
- Compare training stability of models
    

👉 This is VERY publishable if analysis is good.

---

## ✅ Type 3: “New insight / trade-off” → partial-result slice

This is closest to what your 学长 likely did.

### Situation:

During your big project, you discover:

> “Method A works better, BUT only when X happens.”

That alone can be a paper.

### Structure:

- Define scenario
    
- Show phenomenon
    
- Explain why (important!)
    
- Give implication
    

💡 Example:

- “Model A overfits faster than Model B under noisy labels”
    

---

# 🔧 Now: How YOU can actually do it (practical workflow)

Here’s a concrete method you can follow:

---

## Step 1: Decompose your project

Write this down:

```
My project =
1. Problem
2. Method
3. Data
4. Experiments
5. Observations
```

Now ask:

👉 Which part is already “interesting enough alone”?

---

## Step 2: Look for these “paper signals”

During your work, watch for:

### 🚨 Signal A: You made a choice

- Why did you choose method A over B?  
    👉 → That comparison = a paper
    

---

### 🚨 Signal B: Something surprising happened

- Unexpected result  
    👉 → That insight = a paper
    

---

### 🚨 Signal C: You simplified something

- Found an easier way to do X  
    👉 → That method = a paper
    

---

### 🚨 Signal D: You organized messy knowledge

👉 → That = a survey / synthesis paper

---

## Step 3: Turn it into a “minimum publishable unit”

A small paper only needs:

```
1. Clear question
2. One core idea
3. Evidence
4. Conclusion
```

NOT:

- huge system
    
- perfect solution
    
- full project
    

---

# 🧱 Example (very concrete)

Let’s say your research is:

> Using GraphRAG for rare disease diagnosis

---

### Big project:

- build full system
    
- integrate multi-agent + graph
    
- evaluate diagnosis quality
    

---

### Possible “small papers” inside:

#### Paper 1 (Type 2)

> Compare GraphRAG vs normal RAG on medical queries

#### Paper 2 (Type 3)

> GraphRAG fails when graph is sparse — analysis

#### Paper 3 (Type 1)

> A new framework for combining knowledge graph + LLM agents

---

Each one:

- independent
    
- publishable
    
- part of bigger story
    

---

# ⚠️ Important: What NOT to do

Bad slicing:

❌ “Just a piece of code”  
❌ “Incomplete idea without conclusion”  
❌ “No insight, only implementation”

Good slicing:

✅ “Small but meaningful question fully answered”

---

# 🧭 Final thinking model (this is the key takeaway)

After solving a problem, you should start asking:

```
1. What did I learn that others don’t know?
2. Is this insight independent?
3. Can I prove it with experiments or reasoning?
```

If YES → you have a paper.
